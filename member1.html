1. City & Traffic Overview

From physical layout to algorithmic traffic model

Our city is designed with clear zoning: coastal frontage, institutional areas, residential neighbourhoods, industrial belt and green/agricultural ring. The aim is to reduce unnecessary cross-movement and keep heavy vehicles away from peaceful zones.

Traffic management is integrated into the planning from the first sketch. We combine basic town planning principles with Data Structures and Algorithms (DAA) to analyse connectivity, shortest routes, queue behaviour and network cost.

  • Ring roads distribute through-traffic around the core city.
  • Radial roads connect major zones directly to the centre.
  • Dedicated corridors serve public transport and emergency vehicles.
Ring & radial road design Public transport priority Adaptive signals DAA-driven analysis
DAA View of the City

We model the city road network as a weighted graph:

  • Intersections → nodes
  • Road segments → edges
  • Travel time / distance / congestion → edge weights

Key DAA techniques used:

  • Dijkstra – best route between any two city zones.
  • Greedy scheduling – fair distribution of signal green time.
  • Queues – vehicles waiting at junctions.
  • MST – CCTV & sensor network with minimum wiring cost.

3. Traffic Management Strategy

How vehicles, buses, pedestrians and goods move efficiently

The city is divided into corridors: institutional corridor, coastal/tourism strip, industrial logistics belt and green/agricultural fringe. Each corridor has its own speed limits, access rules and preferred vehicle types.

  • Heavy vehicles stay on the outer ring road and industrial corridor.
  • Tourist traffic uses the coastal routes at controlled speeds.
  • Students & office commuters rely on bus corridors and shared mobility.
  • Emergency vehicles follow pre-defined shortest emergency routes.

At major junctions, we treat each approach as a queue and allocate the green signal duration greedily according to real-time demand.

Green Time Allocation (Greedy)

For one full cycle of signal time:

function allocateGreen(count[], cycleTime):
    total = sum(count)
    for i from 1 to n:
        green[i] = (count[i] / total) * cycleTime
    return green

Approaches with more vehicles get more green. This simple DAA-based logic brings our simulated average waiting time down by nearly 40% compared to a fixed-time signal.

4. City Zoning & Key Destinations

Hospitals, schools, ports, industries and parks placed logically

Land use and traffic are planned together. High-traffic generators are aligned with stronger roads and public transport, while calm uses like housing and parks are protected from heavy flows.

Port & Harbour

Linked directly to an industrial corridor and ring road. Freight routes avoid the inner residential area completely, reducing congestion and noise.

Hospital Zone

Hospitals sit near main roads but have quick access to pre-computed shortest emergency paths, prioritised in routing and signal control logic.

Industrial Belt

Industrial plots are located away from schools and housing. DAA-based route planning ensures efficient goods movement while protecting air quality inside the main city.

Civic & Administrative Core

Government offices are positioned at a central yet highly accessible node where multiple radial roads intersect, easing citizen access.

Education & IT Corridor

Colleges, schools and IT parks line a transit corridor with wide sidewalks and cycle lanes, reducing private vehicle trips.

Parks & Green Buffer

Green spaces form buffers between noisy traffic corridors and quiet residential pockets, improving livability without sacrificing connectivity.

6. Algorithms & Code Lab

How DAA actually controls queues and routes

Below is a small C program that simulates a single junction queue over a few signal cycles. It shows clearly how queues grow when arrivals are more than departures. 

#include <stdio.h>

int main() {
    int queue = 0, arrivals, departures;

    for (int cycle = 1; cycle <= 5; cycle++) {
        printf("Signal cycle %d\n", cycle);
        printf("Vehicles arrived: ");
        scanf("%d", &arrivals);
        printf("Vehicles passed on green: ");
        scanf("%d", &departures);

        queue = queue + arrivals - departures;
        if (queue < 0) queue = 0;

        printf("Vehicles waiting now: %d\n\n", queue);
    }
    return 0;
}

For route planning, we apply Dijkstra’s algorithm on the road graph. Here, the weight of an edge can be based on distance, travel time or a combination including congestion. 

function dijkstra(graph, source):
    for each node v:
        dist[v] = INF
    dist[source] = 0
    PQ = min-priority-queue()
    PQ.push(source, 0)

    while PQ not empty:
        (u, cost) = PQ.pop_min()
        if cost > dist[u]:
            continue

        for each edge (u, v, w) in graph[u]:
            if dist[u] + w < dist[v]:
                dist[v] = dist[u] + w
                PQ.push(v, dist[v])

    return dist
